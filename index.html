US Tax Obligations for Foreign Rental Property

As a US citizen you owe the IRS on all foreign rental income regardless of local taxes paid. Framework only — not a substitute for a CPA with international real estate experience.

01

Report All Rental Income

Foreign rental income goes on Schedule E (Form 1040) like domestic property. No threshold, no exclusion. Convert at average annual exchange rate for the year.

02

Foreign Tax Credit — Form 1116

Taxes paid abroad on rental income can offset US liability on the same income, generally preventing double taxation. File Form 1116 annually — requires tracking taxes paid per country and income category.

03

FBAR — FinCEN 114

Foreign bank accounts exceeding $10,000 aggregate at any point during the year require FinCEN 114 by April 15 (auto-extended Oct 15). Willful non-filing: up to $100,000 or 50% of account balance per violation.

04

FATCA — Form 8938

Report foreign financial assets over $50,000 (or $75,000 at any point) if living in the US. Penalty for non-filing: $10,000. Includes foreign bank accounts used for rent collection.

05

Depreciation: 40 Years, Not 27.5

Foreign rental property must be depreciated over 40 years straight-line (vs 27.5 years for US residential). Mandatory — cannot be accelerated. Building value only; land is non-depreciable.

06

Passive Activity Loss Rules

Rental losses are generally "passive" and can only offset passive income unless you qualify as a real estate professional or are an active participant with AGI under $100k–$150k. Unused losses carry forward.

07

Currency Gain / Loss at Sale

Gains are calculated in USD. Currency movements between purchase date and sale date create a separate "Section 988 transaction" gain — taxable even if the property was flat in local currency terms.

08

Estate Tax: Worldwide Assets

US citizens face estate tax on worldwide assets including foreign property. Exemption is $13.99M in 2026 but subject to legislative change. Consult an estate attorney before acquiring multiple properties internationally.

Key CPA questions: Form 8858 (foreign disregarded entity), GILTI if using foreign companies, state tax treatment (California taxes worldwide income). Engage international expertise before your first purchase.

Sequencing across all 25 cities: Tbilisi is the only entry that costs zero Schengen days — study it early regardless of tier. Split and Croatia broadly are the cities most directly rewarded by citizenship resolving (Ministry approval friction disappears entirely). Lyon is the watchlist entry pending French non-resident tax legislation. In the Eastern European tier, the core question is whether you want lower absolute prices (Plovdiv, Catania, Ljubljana, Vilnius) or stronger rental demand and liquidity (Kraków, Wrocław, Sofia/Lozenets). Those are different bets.

On acquisition costs: The gap between Italy/Portugal/France (11–17% true entry cost) and Eastern European markets (3–7%) is large enough to change how many properties the same capital budget can support. On a €1M total budget, the difference between buying in Belgium vs. Estonia is roughly one entire additional property.

On the Italian 9% rate: This is the single most important non-obvious fact in this file. The prima casa exemption (2%) requires Italian residence and is intended for primary-residence purchases by Italian residents. As a US citizen without Italian residency at time of purchase — even with citizenship pending — you pay 9% on resale properties. Bologna and Naples listings look better value on headline price than they actually are once this is factored in. Plan accordingly.

Regulatory currency: STR rules, buyer restrictions, and zone-level freezes move fast — Thessaloniki's freeze extended July 1, 2026, less than a week before this file was compiled. Re-verify zone-level status with a local solicitor before any purchase decision, not just at the city level.
